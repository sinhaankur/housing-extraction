# Case study: a $700,000 Toronto condo, build → year 10

Concrete dollar accounting for a single unit. Build cost ~$560k. First sale at $700k. Resold at year 5 and year 10.

## Extraction table

| Player                                                | Amount             | Notes                                          |
|-------------------------------------------------------|--------------------|------------------------------------------------|
| Developer (first sale)                                | **~$140,000**      | 20% gross margin on $700k sale                 |
| Government (HST + land transfer + dev charges)        | **~$80,000–110,000** | Cumulative across players                    |
| Bank (construction loan + buyer mortgage interest yr 1–5) | **~$95,000**   | Interest on the buyer's mortgage alone         |
| Listing agent + buyer's agent (first resale, year 5)  | **~$35,000**       | 5% of resale price                             |
| Bank again (new mortgage interest on year-5 buyer, yr 1–5) | **~$95,000**  | Fresh frontloaded interest schedule            |
| Government again (land transfer tax on year-5 resale) | **~$22,000**       | Toronto municipal LTT + Ontario provincial LTT |
| Agents at next resale (year 10)                       | **~$38,000**       | Another transaction cycle                      |

## Totals

**Roughly $500,000–$550,000** flows to the four extractor classes in the first 10 years of this unit's life — for a unit that may have cost the developer **$560k to actually construct**.

## What the occupier got

Shelter. Possibly some price appreciation, mostly offset by interest paid to the bank and transaction costs paid to agents + government on entry and (if they sell) exit.

## What this tells us

The physical asset's contribution to occupiers' lives — a place to live — costs ~$560k to produce *once*. Everything beyond that is **value flowing from occupiers to the extractor classes** without any new shelter being created.
