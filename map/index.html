02 — What you actually pay

The sticker is one number. The bill is another.

Walk a single $700,000 Toronto condo through 10 years — purchase, year-5 resale, year-10 resale. Each layer of the bar below is real money leaving the buyer's account (or embedded in the price they paid). Hover or tap for the calculation.

Direct money out Indirect / embedded in price The home itself
The home (≈ $560k)
Construction labour, materials, land, dev profit. The only layer that produces shelter. Paid once, at first sale.
Direct taxes (≈ $144k)
HST on new build (~$50k), land transfer tax × 3 sales (~$45k), property tax × 10 yrs (~$49k). Visible on closing statements.
Indirect taxes (≈ $98k)
HST on materials & services (~$32k embedded), corporate tax on developer profit (~$35k), corporate tax on bank interest (~$24k), development charges & permit fees passed through (~$7k of the build cost line).
Bank interest (≈ $190k)
10 years of mortgage interest across two renewals. Frontloaded — 65–75% of every monthly payment in year 1.
Realtor commissions (≈ $73k)
5% on the year-5 resale, 5% on the year-10 resale. Each is full price × 5%, regardless of whether the agents added any value.
Insurance & fees (≈ $35k)
CMHC mortgage default insurance (~$30k on a 5% down loan), title insurance, legal fees, appraisal, condo fees over 10 yrs (separate from above).
03 — Value over time

A house in 1990 was three things. In 2026 it's five.

Toronto's average price went from $255k (1990) to $1.02M (2026) — but the house didn't get 4× more useful. Below: first the granular year-by-year journey of one representative unit with what drove each move, then the decomposition into the four things that make up today's price.

Hover the line to read each year.
Major event Policy shift Year tick
Real construction cost Land scarcity Currency expansion Financialization premium
Real construction cost
Materials + labour to physically build the unit. Tracks construction cost index (~2.5–3.5%/yr inflation). The only component anchored to physical reality.
Land scarcity
Zoning + demand + restrictive supply. Real in the sense that land is finite — but in Toronto, restrictive zoning is the dominant driver, not absolute land shortage.
Currency expansion
M2 money supply growth above productive GDP. Post-1971 fiat regime — accelerated by post-2008 QE and 2020 COVID expansion. Same house, expanding unit of account.
Financialization premium
The price you pay because housing is now a leveraged asset class. Includes MBS demand, foreign capital channeling (petrodollar recycling), and speculation. Residual after the other three.
