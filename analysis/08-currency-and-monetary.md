# 8. Currency, the petrodollar, and money printing — the fuel under everything else

Everything above this page (developers, agents, banks, financialization) operates *inside* a monetary system. That system is itself an extraction layer — possibly the largest one. House "appreciation" is partly currency debasement chasing the same physical unit.

If you don't see this layer, you'll misread the rest: rising house prices look like wealth creation. With this layer in view, much of the rise is **nominal expansion of the unit of account**, not real growth in the housing stock or its usefulness.

## The chain in one sentence

> Fiat currency expansion → reserve-currency capital flows → bank credit creation → mortgage origination → asset-price inflation in housing → MBS → central-bank purchases of MBS → more reserve creation → loop.

## The pieces

### 1. Bretton Woods ends (1971)
USD comes off gold. Every major currency becomes pure fiat. There is no longer an external anchor on how much money any major central bank can create.

What changes for housing: the unit of account (USD, CAD, GBP, INR, etc.) becomes elastic. House prices measured in that unit can rise indefinitely without the house changing.

### 2. The petrodollar (1974+)
US-Saudi agreement: oil sold worldwide in USD. Oil-exporting surpluses ("petrodollars") get recycled into US Treasuries and US banking system deposits.

What this does:
- Creates **permanent global demand for USD** (everyone needs dollars to buy oil)
- Routes vast foreign surpluses into US bank balance sheets
- Those banks deploy the funding — heavily into mortgage lending and real estate

This is the structural reason US real estate is a global capital sink. The same flow funds construction in cities far outside the US — because USD is the channel and US-dollar-denominated assets are the destination.

### 3. Reserve-currency privilege
Because so many trades, debts, and reserves are USD-denominated, the Federal Reserve effectively exports its monetary policy. When the Fed expands USD supply:
- USD-denominated debt globally cheapens
- Capital flows into USD assets including US real estate
- Other countries' currencies move against USD, which transmits the effect to **their** housing markets too

This is why the **Vancouver, Toronto, London, Sydney, Auckland** housing booms move roughly in sync — they're nodes on the same USD-driven capital network.

### 4. Securitization closes the loop
- Bank originates a mortgage
- Packages it into an MBS (see [06-financialization.md](06-financialization.md))
- Sells the MBS — sometimes to the central bank itself, under QE
- The central bank pays with newly-created reserves
- Those reserves enable more bank lending
- Which originates more mortgages

The Fed, ECB, BoE, BoJ all became major MBS / sovereign-bond buyers after 2008. The **central bank is now structurally a backstop bid for the financialization stack**. This wasn't true before 2008.

### 5. The 2020 demonstration
- US M2 money supply: **+25% in 18 months** (March 2020 – mid 2021). Largest peacetime expansion ever.
- US median home price: **+40%** in roughly the same window.
- Same pattern, smaller magnitudes, in Canada, UK, Australia, NZ.

The houses did not become 40% more useful or 40% scarcer. The currency expanded.

## Country differences trace back to this layer

| Country     | Currency posture                              | Housing-inflation susceptibility |
|-------------|-----------------------------------------------|----------------------------------|
| US          | Reserve currency issuer                       | High — exports inflation, also absorbs capital |
| Canada, UK, Australia, NZ | Floating, deeply integrated with USD | **Very high** — receive USD capital, no buffer |
| Eurozone (most)  | Floating but ECB-managed                 | High but uneven                  |
| Japan       | Strong domestic preference, low yield curve   | **Low** — currency stays domestic; cf. 1.5% mortgage rates |
| Switzerland | Strong franc, capital controls historically   | Moderate — franc strength absorbs some inflation |
| India       | Capital-account restrictions, high rates       | High inflation but partly walled off from USD waves |
| China       | Capital controls; managed yuan                 | Domestic credit cycle dominates; capital partly trapped |

Countries with **stronger monetary sovereignty and currency independence** (Japan, Switzerland) see less housing inflation. Countries fully exposed to USD capital flows see more.

## The petrodollar weakening — and what it implies

The petrodollar system is under measurable stress (oil-yuan settlements, BRICS settlement experiments, central-bank gold buying at multi-decade highs). If USD's reserve role erodes:

- **Less foreign capital channeled into US/Anglo housing** → Anglo housing markets lose a structural bid.
- **More foreign capital seeking alternative stores of value** → gold, BTC, *and other countries' real estate* (the bid moves, doesn't disappear).
- **Higher US rates needed to attract the same capital** → mortgage costs rise, asset prices compress.

The 2022–2026 housing softening in Toronto/Vancouver/Sydney is consistent with this. Hard to disentangle from rate hikes, but the two effects compound.

## The recursive loop in plain terms

Housing prices rise → people borrow more to buy → banks create more deposits to lend → those deposits show up as "money supply" → measured inflation prompts central-bank response → policy choices either reinforce or break the loop.

Crucially: **bank lending creates money**. When a bank originates a $500k mortgage, it doesn't lend out pre-existing deposits — it credits the seller's account with $500k of new deposits and books a $500k loan as an asset. The mortgage market is **the largest single channel of money creation** in modern economies (~70% of broad money creation in the UK, per Bank of England 2014 analysis).

So house-price inflation isn't *just* a consequence of money printing. **Mortgage lending IS the money printing** in most developed economies. The housing market is the machine. The central bank is the backstop. The petrodollar is the global routing.

## Why this matters for the "creating value of something that isn't value" thesis

The financialization layer ([06](06-financialization.md)) showed how derivatives generate revenue without producing shelter. This layer shows that the **denominator itself** — the currency the value is measured in — is expanding faster than the housing stock.

So a unit going from $300k → $700k:
- Maybe ~30% of that is real (better location, real scarcity, genuine improvement)
- Maybe ~70% is currency expansion + leverage + capital flow effects

Which means the "wealth" created by housing appreciation is **mostly a transfer mechanism**, not a creation mechanism:
- From wage-earners (whose income is denominated in the same depreciating unit) to asset-holders
- From later buyers to earlier sellers
- From renters to landlords
- From every productive sector to the FIRE sector (finance, insurance, real estate)

Housing isn't producing the wealth. It's **the receipt printer for the wealth being produced elsewhere — and then captured.**
