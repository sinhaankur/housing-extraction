# 6. Financialization — creating value out of something that isn't value

The thesis: the price of a house bears decreasing relationship to its use as shelter. Most of the price is a claim on **future transactions and future leverage**, not on the building itself.

## Use value vs. exchange value

A house has a **use value**: shelter. One person occupies one unit at a time. A second house can't be lived in twice as hard.

A house also has an **exchange value**: what someone will pay for it. This is unbounded — it scales with whatever buyers can borrow, which scales with whatever rates and incomes permit.

These two values **diverged decisively** after 1980. Inflation-adjusted Toronto house prices roughly tripled between 1995 and 2022. The houses didn't get three times more useful. What grew was the financial claim wrapped around the same physical structure.

## The derivative stack

Each layer claims value off the underlying loan without adding shelter:

```
┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│ Layer 4: Credit default swaps                    │  bets ON other people's bets ON the MBS
├──────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│ Layer 3: CDOs                                    │  bundles of MBS slices, sold to investors
├──────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│ Layer 2: Mortgage-backed securities (MBS)        │  pools of mortgages turned into tradeable bonds
├──────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│ Layer 1: The mortgage itself                     │  bank's claim on the borrower's future interest
├──────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│ Layer 0: The house                               │  the only layer that provides shelter
└──────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

Each upper layer is **purely a claim on the layers below**. None of them adds a square foot of housing. But each of them generates fees, spreads, and trading profits — the financial industry's revenue from housing without building any.

In the 2008 crisis, the **notional value of housing-linked derivatives** exceeded the total value of the underlying US housing stock by an order of magnitude. The bets on the bets dwarfed the thing being bet on.

## Fictitious capital

The term (David Harvey, after Marx) describes value that exists as a claim on **future** value rather than present production. A 30-year mortgage is a claim on 30 years of the borrower's future labour-income. The bank books it as an asset today.

Securitization compounds this — the MBS is a claim on the mortgage which is a claim on future interest which is a claim on future labour. By the time the security is trading at layer 3 or 4, there is no actual house anywhere near the transaction.

## The empty-unit phenomenon

If exchange value dominates, **occupancy stops mattering**. The unit's job is to *store value*, not *house someone*.

Patterns observed:
- **Vancouver, Toronto**: foreign-buyer waves in 2014–2018 produced significant empty-condo stock; Vancouver's empty-homes tax (2017) was a direct response.
- **London prime central**: estimated 15–30% of ultra-prime units in Kensington / Chelsea unoccupied or used as occasional pieds-à-terre.
- **Manhattan ultra-luxury**: similar pattern at 432 Park Ave, One57.
- **Mumbai**: estimated **11 million vacant urban units** per 2011 census in a country with severe housing shortage. Vacancy concentrated in the investor-driven upper segments.

These units aren't "housing" by any functional definition. They're **safety-deposit boxes that happen to have plumbing**. But they show up in housing supply statistics, propping up the illusion that the market is producing shelter.

## Why this is the deepest layer of the scam

The other extractor classes ([developers](01-developers.md), [agents](02-agents.md), [banks](03-banks.md), [governments](04-governments.md)) all take a slice off real transactions. Financialization is more radical: it generates a separate, larger revenue stream **without any transaction in shelter occurring at all**.

A pension fund buying an MBS tranche generates revenue for the bank, the rating agency, the broker, and the custodian. No one moved into a house. No one moved out. The building doesn't even know it happened.

This is the precise mechanism by which "value" is created out of something that isn't value. The shelter is incidental. The house is the **excuse** for the financial activity, not its purpose.

## What this implies

- **Housing "supply" measured in built units overstates real supply**, because a meaningful fraction is functionally absent (vacant / underused / held purely as asset).
- **House prices reflect financial conditions** (rates, leverage, capital flows) at least as much as housing fundamentals (incomes, demographics, construction cost).
- **Crashes are inevitable**: a derivative stack of arbitrary height eventually exceeds the load-bearing capacity of the physical asset beneath it. 2008 was the demonstration, not the exception.
- **Reform that touches only one layer doesn't work.** Banning foreign buyers (touches occupancy), capping commissions (touches agents), raising rates (touches banks) — each addresses one slice. Until the *exchange-value over use-value* inversion is unwound, the system regenerates the same dynamics through whichever slice is still open.
