# 5. Patterns and trends — the system is consolidating

## Pattern A: Vertical integration is accelerating

Major developers now own in-house brokerage, in-house mortgage origination, and in-house property management — capturing the developer margin, the agent commission, and the financing spread on the same unit.

| Region   | Vertically integrated players                                          |
|----------|------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| Canada   | **Tridel**, **Daniels**, **Brookfield** (effectively owns parts of all four layers — development, brokerage via Royal LePage parent, lending, property management) |
| India    | **Lodha**, **Godrej**, **DLF**                                         |
| US       | Various builder-broker-lender combos (e.g. Lennar / Eagle Home Mortgage) |

The trend: the developer, broker, and lender used to be **three separate businesses competing** for slices. Increasingly they're **divisions of the same parent**. The customer (buyer or renter) faces one integrated extraction machine instead of three competing ones.

## Pattern B: Institutional landlords and REITs

Same dynamic on the rental + holding side.

In the US:
- **BlackRock, Invitation Homes, and similar institutional buyers** now own ~3% of single-family homes
- Far higher share of new apartment supply
- They are buyer, landlord, and eventual seller — capturing rental yield while waiting for capital gains, then taking agent-side savings when liquidating in bulk

## The structural reason the "scam" instinct registers something real

The asset itself doesn't keep generating value. **The transactions generate value for everyone except the occupier.**

- Developers earn once
- Governments and agents earn each time it changes hands
- Banks earn continuously throughout the life of every mortgage written against it

The system is optimized for **transaction volume and asset price appreciation**, not for housing people. Anything that increases turnover (resales, refinances, assignments) increases the extracted value pool — even though the underlying physical unit is unchanged.

## Implications worth tracking

1. **More turnover → more extraction.** Policies that increase mobility (relocation incentives, market liquidity reforms) can paradoxically transfer more wealth from occupiers to extractors.
2. **Mortgage term structure matters enormously.** A 25-year held mortgage extracts ~half what 5 successive 5-year mortgages do, on the same property. Country-level differences in renewal frequency (Canada 5-year resets vs US 30-year fixed) shape lifetime bank capture.
3. **Vertical integration changes the regulatory frame.** Antitrust around bundled commissions (Sitzer-Burnett) addresses *one* slice. It doesn't touch the developer-broker-lender bundle.
4. **AI / portal disintermediation only attacks the agent layer.** Banks and developers are structurally insulated. So even successful broker disruption (Zillow, OpenDoor, Redfin) leaves 3 of 4 extractor classes untouched.
