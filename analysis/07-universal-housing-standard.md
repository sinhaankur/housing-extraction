# 7. There should be one universal housing standard

If a unit is going to count as housing — count toward "supply," qualify for mortgage insurance, be sold as a residence — it should meet a single, defensible standard for what housing *is*.

Right now the standards are fragmented, mostly captured by industry, and weakest exactly where capture is most profitable (luxury micro-units, investor-targeted shoebox condos, sub-spec rentals).

## Existing standards (what we'd be unifying)

| Standard                         | Scope                | Strength                          | Weakness                       |
|----------------------------------|----------------------|-----------------------------------|--------------------------------|
| **UN-Habitat "Adequate Housing"** | Global rights framing | 7 criteria, principled            | Aspirational, no enforcement   |
| **WHO Housing & Health Guidelines** | Global health         | Evidence-based on thermal, mold, crowding | Health-only, no tenure / cost  |
| **Passivhaus**                   | Energy performance   | Rigorous, certifiable             | Energy-only                    |
| **UK Technical Housing Standards (Nationally Described Space Standard)** | England           | Minimum floor area per occupant   | Optional adoption; widely waived |
| **NYC Housing Maintenance Code** | NYC, habitability    | Enforceable                       | Local, reactive                |
| **Japan minimum dwelling size guidance** | Japan, ~25m² studio | Codified                          | Guidance, not enforced strictly |

No single standard covers **all six** of the load-bearing dimensions below.

## The proposed unified standard — six dimensions

A unit must clear all six to count as housing. Failure in any dimension means it counts as **asset**, not housing — taxed, regulated, and reported separately.

### 1. Habitability
- Minimum floor area per occupant (≥ ~14 m²/person typical, lower for studios)
- Natural light: at least one window per habitable room, minimum glazing ratio
- Ventilation: openable to outside air OR mechanical ventilation meeting WHO indoor-air thresholds
- Thermal: maintainable at 18–24°C year-round without unaffordable energy cost
- Sanitation: private (not shared with non-household) toilet, bathing, kitchen
- Structural: meets seismic / fire / weatherproofing codes appropriate to region

### 2. Energy & emissions
- Operational energy ≤ Passivhaus or equivalent local performance target
- Building envelope durable to climate scenarios for at least its intended lifespan
- No fossil-fuel-only heating in new construction (heat pumps / district heat / electric default)

### 3. Services & location
- Within reasonable walking distance OR transit access to: school, primary healthcare, food retail, employment cluster
- Not in a known flood/fire/seismic exclusion zone except with explicit derating
- Access to safe water and connected sanitation

### 4. Affordability anchor
- Total monthly housing cost (rent OR mortgage + tax + utilities) ≤ 30% of regional median household income for the unit size, **at the time of sale or lease**
- Units that fail this don't lose the right to exist — but they lose preferential mortgage treatment, lose claim to "housing supply" statistics, and pay an asset-class tax

### 5. Security of tenure
- Owner-occupiers: protection against foreclosure for short-term payment shock (e.g. 6-month workout window before forced sale)
- Renters: minimum lease term, indexed-cap rent increases, just-cause eviction
- No tenure form (rental, co-op, leasehold, fee simple) gets *less* protection than another

### 6. Occupancy
- The unit must be **occupied as a primary or genuinely-shared residence** for at least N months/year (e.g. 6 months) to count toward supply
- Below the threshold: classified as asset/store-of-value, taxed accordingly (the Vancouver / Paris model, scaled up)

## Why occupancy is the keystone

Of the six, occupancy is the lever that most directly **uncouples housing from financialization**. As long as an empty unit counts as housing supply, the financial system can:
- Inflate "supply" statistics with units no one lives in
- Generate transactions, MBS, derivatives, etc. against vacant collateral
- Suppress political pressure for actual building

If the definition of housing requires occupancy, vacant units become a distinct asset class (which they functionally already are), and the **shortage gets measured honestly** for the first time.

## What this standard pushes back against

- **Investor-grade micro-units** that hit floor-area minimums on a technicality. The natural-light, ventilation, and services criteria would knock most out.
- **Luxury vacancy** (Manhattan, London, Vancouver, Mumbai). Occupancy threshold reclassifies these.
- **Sub-spec rentals** (basements without egress, illegal partitions). Habitability dimension already covers, but enforcement is the gap.
- **Energy-leaky new builds** sold at a premium. Energy dimension blocks.
- **Far-flung commuter sprawl with no services**. Services & location dimension blocks.

## How this would integrate with the rest of this repo

| Standard dimension fails | Consequence in the housing-extraction frame                            |
|--------------------------|------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| Occupancy                | Unit is asset, not housing. Banks lose mortgage-insurance eligibility. Counted out of supply. |
| Affordability anchor     | Loses preferential tax treatment ([governments](04-governments.md))    |
| Habitability             | Loses ability to be rented or sold as residence                        |
| Energy / services        | Loses preferential financing rates                                     |

The point isn't to ban anything. It's to **stop pretending** that asset-class units, vacant units, and sub-spec units are the same product as housing for people. Once they're separated, every other extractor in this repo faces a different incentive structure.

## Open question

Should this be:
- **(a)** a soft standard (disclosure label — "this unit clears the universal housing standard / doesn't"), or
- **(b)** a hard regulatory standard (mortgage insurance, tax treatment, and "supply" statistics legally gated on it)?

(a) is easier to adopt and gives buyers/voters information. (b) actually changes the system.

A useful trilogy-style prototype would be (a) implemented as a public certification + map overlay, with (b) modelled as a policy simulation.
